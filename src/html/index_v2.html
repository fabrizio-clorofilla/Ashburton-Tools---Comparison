    Volatility Volatility: The degree to which a given security, fund, or index rapidly changes. It is calculated as the degree of deviation from the norm for that type of investment over a given time period. The higher the volatility, the riskier the security tends to be. Sharpe Ratio Sharpe Ratio: A measure of risk-adjusted return. It is calculated by dividing the difference between the return of a given portfolio and the risk-free rate by the standard deviation of the portfolio returns. The risk-free rate is a hypothetical return of an investment with zero risk. The Sharpe ratio is useful to investors as it shows return (excess over the risk-free rate) per unit of risk or the adjustment of investment performance for the risk taken by the fund manager. Beta Beta: A measure of the sensitivity of a portfolio’s or asset’s return relative to the return of the relevant index (usually the stockmarket’s main share index). A beta of less (more) than 1.0 would suggest lower (higher) volatility than the index. Investors look to higher beta investments for higher levels of risk relative to the index. Conversely, a lower beta investment suggests a lower level of risk and thus lower expected returns than the index. Duration Duration: A measure of the sensitivity of a fixed income security, also called a bond, or bond fund to changes in interest rates. The longer a bond or bond fund’s duration, the more sensitive it is to interest rate movements.
